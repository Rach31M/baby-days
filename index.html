⏰Timing tip
Leave right after a feed
A fed, winded baby is your best travelling companion. Time your departure just after a feed and you'll likely get a full outing before the next one is due.
💡 Give yourself twice as long as you think. The first trip out is the hardest — it gets easier every single time.
🧘Mindset
Lower the bar completely
Getting out of the door and back counts as a complete success. You don't need a destination, a plan, or to stay out long.
💡 Pick somewhere low-pressure and walkable for your first few trips — a park bench, a slow loop round the block, a coffee shop.

You are not the first person to wonder how to shower
These are the practical questions every new parent has and nobody talks about. You are doing brilliantly.
🚿
How to shower safely alone
One of the most common worries for new mums
▾
1
Time it after a feed. A fed, winded baby gives you the best window — usually 45–90 minutes of settled time.
2
Put baby somewhere safe first. A bouncy chair on the bathroom floor, play mat, or their cot. Safe and flat is all that matters.
3
Bring the bouncy chair into the bathroom. Outside the shower door so you can maintain eye contact and talk to them throughout.
4
If they cry, that's okay. 5 minutes of crying in a safe place is not harmful. You need to function.
Key rule: Never leave baby on a raised surface unattended — sofa, changing table, bed. Always on the floor or in their cot.
💚 By week 8–10 most babies happily sit in a bouncy chair while you shower — the sound of water is often calming.
🍽️
Eating properly when you're alone
You cannot pour from an empty cup
▾
1
One-handed food is your friend. Toast, bananas, cheese, yogurt, cereal bars. Stock these rather than relying on cooking.
2
Eat when baby eats. During a feed you often have one hand free. Have snacks within reach of your feeding spot always.
3
Hydration — especially if breastfeeding. Keep a large water bottle next to your feeding spot. Thirst feels identical to exhaustion at this stage.
💚 Eating and drinking enough directly affects your energy, milk supply and mood. It is not selfish — it is essential.
😴
Resting safely when you're alone
Sleep deprivation is a genuine health issue
▾
1
Put baby in their safe sleep space and lie down nearby. Even 20 minutes of eyes-closed rest is restorative.
2
Never sleep on the sofa with a baby. Sofa sleeping with an infant is one of the highest risk situations for sudden infant death — even accidentally drifting off.
3
If you feel you might fall asleep while feeding, move to your bed on a firm surface with no loose bedding near baby. Safer than the sofa.
If dangerously exhausted, call a friend, family member or health visitor. There is no shame — exhaustion at this level is a health issue.
🧘
When it feels overwhelming
Managing big emotions alone with a crying baby
▾
1
Put baby down safely and step away. A few minutes of crying in a safe cot does no harm. Coming back calmer is better than staying and spiralling.
2
Text someone. Even 'today is hard' to a friend matters. Isolation makes hard days feel unsurvivable.
3
If most days feel this way, speak to your GP. Postnatal anxiety and depression are health conditions, not character flaws, and treatment works.
Never shake a baby. If you feel you might lose control, put baby down safely, leave the room and call someone immediately. Cry-sis: 08451 228 669.
🍼
Making a bottle safely alone
When baby is crying and hungry and you're by yourself
▾
1
Put baby somewhere safe first. A crying baby is a safe baby. You need both hands free.
2
Keep a flask of boiled water ready. 70°C+ water in a vacuum flask means you can make a bottle in under a minute without waiting for a kettle.
3
Pre-measure formula in a dispenser. A few pounds from any supermarket — pre-measured portions ready to add without struggling one-handed.
4
Never microwave formula. It creates hot spots that can burn baby's mouth even if the bottle feels cool outside.
💚 Ready-to-feed cartons (just open and pour) are more expensive but brilliant for night feeds and hard moments — zero preparation at all.
🤱
Breastfeeding positions that work solo
Managing without a second pair of hands
▾
1
Set up your feeding station before baby wakes. Water, phone, snack, muslin, remote — all within arm's reach.
2
Side-lying feeding. Lie on your side with baby facing you at breast height. Brilliant for night feeds — no sitting up required.
3
Rugby hold for latch difficulties. Tuck baby under your arm like a rugby ball — gives much more control without needing help.
4
National Breastfeeding Helpline: 0300 100 0212. Free, 9:30am–9:30pm every day. They talk you through positions on the phone.
🥣
Weaning safely alone (from ~6 months)
Managing mess and safety when it's just you
▾
1
Always within arm's reach. Never leave a weaning baby alone with food — even for a few seconds.
2
Know gagging vs choking. Gagging is noisy — baby in control, red-faced. Choking is silent — baby pale or blue. Act immediately for choking.
3
Strip to a nappy for meals with a splash mat under the high chair — cleanup takes 2 minutes not 20.

When baby won't stop crying
A step-by-step approach to try
▾
1
Check the basics. Fed? Winded? Nappy clean? Too hot or cold? Overtired? These cover the vast majority of causes.
2
Try motion. Walking on your shoulder, bouncy chair jiggled with your foot, or a sling. Rhythmic movement calms the vestibular system.
3
Try white noise. Extractor fan, shower running, or a white noise app at moderate volume. Womb-like sounds calm many babies quickly.
4
Change the environment. Going outside — even onto a doorstep — often interrupts the crying cycle. Cool air works surprisingly often.
5
Skin to skin as a reset. Undress baby to their nappy and hold against your bare chest. Regulates their nervous system.

Getting baby to sleep alone
When there's no one to take turns with
▾
1
Watch for the sleep window. First yawn or eye rub is your cue. Overtired babies are much harder to settle.
2
Walk + shush + jiggle. Upright on your shoulder, gentle rhythmic patting, steady shushing. Slow everything down as they drowse.
3
The transfer. Lower bottom-first, then back, then head last. Keep your hand on their chest for 30–60 seconds after.
4
A sling for contact naps. Hands free while baby naps on you. Follow TICKS guidelines for safe babywearing.
💚 There is no single right way. What works often changes week to week. That is normal.
🚪
Answering the door or doing a quick task
Safely leaving baby for a few minutes
▾
1
Always put baby down first. Flat, firm surface — play mat on the floor, bouncy chair on the floor, or cot. Never unattended on a sofa or raised surface.
2
A play mat in the hallway next to the front door is perfectly safe for babies who can't yet roll.
3
It's okay to not answer. No delivery or visitor is more important than your baby's safety.
💚 A crying baby in a safe place is safe. You can take 2–3 minutes to answer the door, use the loo, or make a drink.
🏠
Keeping the house ticking alone
Managing basics without burning out
▾
1
One task per nap. Pick just one thing. Some naps that one thing is resting. That is valid.
2
Bouncy chair in every room. A safe spot in the kitchen, bathroom and living room means you can do small things without carrying baby everywhere.
3
Ask for specific help. 'Could you bring dinner on Tuesday?' is better than 'let me know if you need anything'. Make it easy for people.
💚 The house does not need to be tidy. Your baby does not care about the laundry. This season is temporary.
🌙
Surviving night feeds alone
Making the hard hours manageable
▾
1
Set up a night station. Everything within arm's reach — nappies, wipes, formula/muslin, water, phone, a dim lamp. No big lights on.
2
Keep it low light and boring. Bright lights signal daytime to a developing body clock. Night feeds should be dull for both of you.
3
Use a red night light. Red wavelengths don't suppress melatonin like white or blue light — a cheap plug-in red light is a game changer.
💚 Night feeds are temporary. Every one you do is keeping your baby healthy and growing — even at 3am.
